The Odyssey

A Long-Scrolling Story

The Cicones & Lotus Eaters

After their victory in the decade-long Trojan War, Odysseus and his fleet of twelve ships set sail for their homeland, Ithaca. However, the journey home proves to be anything but simple. The gods, particularly Poseidon, are angered by the Greeks' arrogance, and they vow to make his passage a torment. Their first stop at the land of the Cicones results in a bloody battle after the men foolishly linger to plunder and feast, leading to a costly counter-attack. Fleeing the Cicones, they are thrown off course by a furious storm and land on the island of the Lotus-Eaters. There, several of Odysseus's men eat the enchanted lotus flower, which plunges them into a state of blissful apathy, making them forget all desire to return home. Odysseus must forcibly drag these men back to the ships and tie them down, setting sail once more. This encounter is the first of many trials that test his leadership and resolve, demonstrating that the dangers on his journey are not just monsters, but also the seductive pull of oblivion and forgetfulness.

The Cyclops

Seeking supplies, Odysseus and a dozen of his best men enter a large, well-stocked cave, which is soon revealed to be the lair of Polyphemus, a monstrous, one-eyed Cyclops and son of Poseidon. The giant returns with his flock, rolls a massive boulder across the cave entrance, and traps the men inside. He scoffs at their pleas for hospitality and promptly eats two of the men, continuing to feast on them each day. Trapped and desperate, Odysseus devises a cunning plan. He offers the Cyclops a skin of powerful, undiluted wine. When the giant is drunk and sleepy, he asks for Odysseus's name, who replies, "Nobody." Once Polyphemus collapses, Odysseus and his remaining men drive a massive, fire-hardened olive stake into the monster's single eye, blinding him. The Cyclops roars in agony, and when other giants on the island call out to ask who is harming him, he bellows, "Nobody is killing me!" Hearing this, the others assume he is merely ill and leave. To escape, Odysseus ties his men to the bellies of the giant's sheep. When the blind Polyphemus lets his flock out to graze, he feels only their backs, allowing the men to slip out undetected. In a final, fatal act of pride, Odysseus taunts the Cyclops from his ship, revealing his true name. Polyphemus prays to his father, Poseidon, who curses Odysseus to wander the sea for years, ensuring his journey home will be filled with suffering and loss.

Circe's Island

Following their harrowing escape from the Cyclops, Odysseus and his crew land on the island of Aeaea, home to the beautiful and dangerous enchantress, Circe. A scouting party, led by Eurylochus, discovers her palace. Hearing her enchanting song, all the men except the suspicious Eurylochus enter. Circe welcomes them with a feast, but the food is laced with a magic potion. After they eat, she strikes them with her wand, transforming them all into pigs, though they retain their human minds. Eurylochus flees back to the ship and warns Odysseus, who bravely sets out alone to confront the goddess. On the way, he is met by the messenger god Hermes, who gives him a holy herb called moly. This plant makes him immune to Circe's magic. When Circe offers Odysseus the drugged wine and he remains unchanged, she is stunned. He draws his sword, and the terrified goddess swears an oath not to harm him. She then restores his men to their human form. Odysseus and his crew end up staying on her island for a full year, with Circe becoming his ally and lover, eventually giving him crucial advice for the next stages of his journey, including his descent into the Underworld.

The Sirens

Warned by Circe, Odysseus knows he must next sail past the island of the Sirens, deadly creatures who appear as beautiful women (or sometimes bird-like women) whose irresistible song lures sailors to their doom. Any ship that hears their call is drawn onto the sharp rocks, and the sailors are killed. To survive, Odysseus follows Circe's precise instructions. He takes a large block of beeswax, softens it, and plugs the ears of every man on his ship so they are deaf to the magical song. However, Odysseus himself is consumed by curiosity and wishes to be the only man to hear the Sirens' song and live. He orders his men to tie him with heavy ropes to the ship's mast. He gives them one strict command: no matter how much he begs, pleads, or threatens, they are not to untie him. As they sail past, the Sirens' song washes over Odysseus, promising him infinite wisdom and knowledge. He is driven mad with desire, screaming and straining against the ropes to be set free, but his faithful crew only binds him tighter. They row past the island in grim silence until the song fades, at which point they safely unplug their ears and release their tormented captain.

Home at Last

After surviving Calypso's island, the shipwreck from Poseidon, and the hospitality of the Phaeacians, Odysseus finally sets foot on Ithaca, 20 years after he first left. He is alone, having lost all his ships and all his men. The goddess Athena, his patron, disguises him as an old, wretched beggar to protect him. In his absence, his palace has been overrun by over a hundred arrogant suitors, all competing to marry his wife, Penelope, and seize his kingdom, all while feasting on his livestock and abusing his servants. In his beggar's disguise, Odysseus is able to enter his own home, where he is mocked and abused by the suitors, allowing him to see who has remained loyal. He secretly reveals himself to his son, Telemachus, and together they devise a plan. Penelope, guided by Athena, announces a contest: she will marry any man who can string Odysseus's great bow and shoot an arrow clean through twelve axe heads. All the suitors try and fail, unable to even bend the bow. The beggar asks for a turn, and to the suitors' shock, he strings it with ease and makes the impossible shot. At that moment, he sheds his disguise, and alongside Telemachus and a few loyal servants, he slaughters every last suitor, finally cleansing his home and reclaiming his throne.
